Edouard as an author

My research has identified that Edouard – during the 1920s and 1930s - wrote a number of books , booklets and ‘pamphlets’ on Bolshevism, the Russian people and, showing his roots as an economic commentator, on economic trade (and in particular Anglo-Russian trade).

I have located the following [eight] publications from sources around the world (libraries, rare book shops etc) and the physical archive I have constructed has editions and copies of these books.

The table below provides a snapshot profile of the publications followed by my own review of each in turn.

Facts about Bolshevism

A short, 15 page, pamphlet written by Edouard in 1919 in his position as General Secretary of The Russian League of Freedom1 - costing threepence.

A number of parts of this document have been included in separate parts in issues of The Russian.

It starts with a very gloomy and dismal preface written by Edouard painting a picture of Bolshevik Russia with poverty, hunger, sickness and despair. He almost pleads for England to rescue Russia and not let it die.

From the Strand in London he ‘longs to fly over my stricken homeland and convey to my people the message of hope …….. oh! my motherland?!1

What is Bolshevism?

Bolshevism is in translation “moreism” derived from the word “bolshe” more. It is the theory of the vagabond, as against Socialism the theory of the worker.

“The Bolshevik does not wish to produce, but only to enjoy and possess, and his solution is to confiscate that which others produce”.

Freedom of Association

Under a Bolshevik decree all meetings, societies and unions have to meet certain criteria and registration, otherwise they will be considered “counter-revolutionary organisations”.

Red Terror

Repeats announcements by the Central Executive Committee (2nd November & 31st October 1919) which list a whole series of people who have been shot as counter-revolutionaries including many high ranking military personnel, ex Ministers and Counts.

[Interesting to note some of the names: Urussoff, Kolosnoff, Karganoff, Bogaroff, Kolvakoff, Volkoff, Popoff… many names ending in ‘off’.]

Bolshevik Power

What keeps the Bolsheviks in power? … even when there is neither bread nor peace in Russia ?!

Postulated that the Bolshevism took advantage of the situation that the masses could not continue to survive the impact of WW1 and promised them both peace and bread. They incited them to revolt against the “bourgeoise”

At the same time provocative agitation was carried on by German spies and agents.

Edouard argues that in fact Bolshevism wields power by terror even without the support of the people!

They copy every method used by Czarism – in this case the Red Guard, the creation of a large bureaucracy, commissions for stealing State money, torture and blackmail, the control of the Press …

The Extraordinary Commission

The ‘Tchrezvytchayka’ the Extraordinary Commission for fighting counter-revolution, speculation and sabotage – effectively the successor to the Okhranka (Czars secret police).

As well as arrests and executions it was also responsible for the control of frontiers, stations and watching over villages with the help of the Committee of Village Poverty.

Eventually subject to the authority of the Soviet the Commission’s mission was to either shoot or imprison any individuals who were reported to them by agents and spies.

Health & Welfare

Edouard reports that the hospitals of Petrrograd and Moscow have lost their medical staff owing to the impossible conditions and controls exercised over them. Vital medicines and surgical instruments have been stolen.

The country has taken to all kinds of mad experiments leading to high death rates, especially amongst children, typhus and other deadly diseases.

The so called “democratisation” of the medical profession has resulted in hospital porters being elected as doctors and other lower staff able to over-rule professional staff!

Land and Industry

Under the first land decree instead of land being given to the poor landless peasants it was taken by the peasantry already rich who found it easier to actually work the land of the former estate owners – thus an increasing amount of land actually remained untiled!

Further, the peasants could not manage these bigger estates and did not now have access to suitable machinery – all leading to a fall in production yields. Forests were felled and even burned to increase arable land …. The sugar, starch and wine industries were all ruined by these measures.

Still greater were the losses in the mining and manufacturing industries.

“the Bolshevik policy has proved a decided failure”

Interestingly Edouard suggests that the peasantry, les exhausted than the town population, show more energy in their opposition to Bolshevism with regular uprisings and revolts having to be put down by the Red Guards.

Bolshevik Russia

A 192 page book on Bolshevik Russia by G.E.Raine1 in collaboration with Edouard Luboff, LL.D first published in 1920 by Nisbet & Co. Ltd.

The aim of the book was to explore and examine Russia of 1920 - the peoples and their characteristics; their spiritual, social and industrial setting. To look at Marxian Socialism and its impact on Russia - but from the start the authors state that 'we do not for an instant condone, or do anything but deplore, the horrors of the Revolution.' The Red Flag has been followed by the Red Guards and the Red Terror, and there has been blood all the way.

The introduction talks about the importance of not viewing Russians as wholly European, the Russian problem is a whole-world problem – “the Soviet state must be studied in the fullest detail as if it is good then it will not end in Russia and if it be evil it must not begin in any other land.”

The Englishman has a traditional disinclination for concerning himself with foreign countries. He has been accustomed to sum up their national characteristics in a phrase - usually more or less offensive and relegate them to their place on the map. Hitherto he has known little of world history and cared less. For him, his own has been the only country that counts.

The aim of the book is consider the results as much if not more than the machinery of the Revolution.

The challenges are that any visit to Russia is influenced by the over bearing hospitality, the opposition press has been suppressed and silenced, the facts provided by the State are questionable – there is not so much Free Speech as Free Prison !!

The book is divided into the following chapters:

Chapter Heading Precis
Russia and the Russians “Hands off Russia!” “Save Russia for Democracy” – so goes up the Soviet Socialist cry
Aim to show that the Force required to achieve the Revolution is nothing compared to the Force that “is called for every hour of every day to impose the Revolution on the [so called] ‘emancipated’.
Russia is vast – 8.5 miliion sq miles of which 6.3million were in Asia and the Caucasus. Circa 175milion people – split 86% villagers and peasants and 14% town-dwellers. ^5% Russians, 11% Turco-Tartars, 6% Poles, 4.5% Finns, 3% Jews, 2.4% Lithuanians and 1.6% Germans. By the law of the Russian Empire te people are divided into four classes: The Clergy, the Nobility, Burghers and Peasants – with an almost negligible middle class!
The Birth and Growth of the Bolsheviks “Bolshevism was born in London in the summer of 1903 – on the occasion of the meeting of the Congress of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party in the East End. – here the first paragraph of the Party Rules was discussed. – Lenin was present at this meeting. – Bolsheviki – the majority.
Two years later in 1905 ban attempted revolution in Russia failed but in May 1906 a Congress was held in Stockholm to formulatethe basis for a fresh revolutionary struggle. – this sought to unite all the revolutionary organisations and to agree on a programme. – all the forces of Russia were to be linked up against the Tsar.
Lenin and Labour in Russia Lenin now needed to establish himself in power and he used to form of Decrees to achieve this – resulting in the Decree on Compulsory Labour (1920) which laid down some very draconian arrangements (rules/principles) – effectively stating that every person must work for the State including the Labour Force in the Military Services.
It repudiates the principle of “freedom of labour”.
“..he who will not work shall not eat, or vote, and labour is compulsory”
Russian Finance before and after the Revolution Refers to the book written by MN Nordman entitled Peace Problems and Russia’s Economics - before the Revolution he claims that the financial position of Russia was far from hopeless with Russia still possessing considerable resources..
By the time of the first Bolshevik budget he estimates that the deficit was 14.75billion roubles with only about 1/6 of State expenditure covered by revenues!!
Russian Industry Before the Revolution it is argued that Russia had vast mineral wealth and a healthy agricultural industry – with a number of very healthy industries such as flax, Hemp, tobacco, fruit and timber.
“A terrible transformation then took place” – the chapter reports this under the report of M Rykoff submitted to the Congress of National Economic Councils in January 1920 and is reproduced here in full.
Further facts with regard to the paralysis of Russian industries under Bolshevism is quoted from M Nordman’s book Peace Problems.
The Armed Proletariat The Russian Trade Union movement was in its infancy in 1918 but between April and August 1917 4 million new members were enrolled into the movement!
Lenin believed that to achieve the stated aims there we a need for an “armed proletariat” but how does that square with Lenin demanding to control the armed forces to ‘impose on the working-classes his personal views as to what is for their own good’ !!
The chapter quotes from a letter from LC Ward a Labour MP in 1918 – “…both Tsarism and Bolshevism try to destroy the intellectual elements of their country”
Lenin did not like criticism of course and whe he found himself attacked by the British delegation in June 1920 he responded in full – quoted verbatim in this chapter
The Bolshevik Threat to the World In the beginning it is argued Bolshevism was intensely pacifist looking to find peace for Russia but very quickly it became militaristic and aggressively offensive.
It now looks for World domination – to roll it out to buffer states, Poland and to get Germany in its grip!
In particular it wants to destroy the capitalist Great Britain – to create an environment when Britain itself has a successful Revolution.
The Russian Soviet Constitution Reproduced in full in this chapter – adopted by the fifth all Russian congress of Soviets at its sitting of 10th July 1918.
“Russia is declared a Republic of Soviets of Workers’, Soldiers, and Peasants’ Delegates. All the central and local power is vested in these Soviets”.

A Red Year – The Soviets in 1926

A 136 page book written by Edouard, published in March 1927 by the Anglo-Russian Press.

It is described on the title page as a record of Soviet life activities and intrigues at home and abroad during 1926.

Note: It refers to the fact that the memorandum entitled “The Soviets in 1925” was received very favourably by the Press and its readers, but owing to its form and price its circulation was restricted. The form has therefore been changed to hopefully increase the circulation potential.

It is full of fascinating quotes and pen and pencil drawings of the principal leaders.

As this is such a substantial document I propose to summarise the Author’s Foreword and to highlight in brief detail the subject matters covered in the various chapter headings.

Author’s Foreword (February 1927)

The material from which the book was compiled is derived entirely from official sources – trying to avoid his own partisanship coming through.

The picture painted is of Russia in her ordinary working clothes and manners, undisturbed by shyness or by the desire to impress foreign visitors.

The picture is gloomy and sordid … Soviet policy and activities were predominantly anti-British.

Edouard claims that:

“…. he is not a politician, either in the Russian or British sense. He belongs to no Party and subscribes to no programme; his sole desire is to show that the Soviet system, both politically and economically is unsound, not because I bear any malice to Soviet leaders, but because the results have turned a progressive country into a backward country.

For years my work was devoted to the extension of Russian industry, commerce and finance in co-operation with this country [Britain]. I fought for modern ideas of commerce because I saw that, by coming into line with progressive European countries, Russia could become wealthier and happier.

This book is, therefore, not “a plea for Czarism”, an accusation levelled against me by a Labour reviewer of my previous efforts, nor is it “a hazardous throw for intervention”, another accusation.

It is to show that “antagonism of classes” must lead to “antagonism of nations” and that all antagonism is a social and economic evil.”

He concludes by saying that he hopes to issue another review next year!1

Chapter Heading Precis
Under the Soviet Star Postulates that the “Birth of Bolshevism” actually took place in 1903 at a meeting in London, called by Lenin, of 36 representatives of local Russian revolutionary organisations.
Highlights that in 1926 there were only actually just over a million Communists out of a population of 143 million.
The Party is the ruling body but the Politbureau has the supreme power vested in it.
This chapter then provides a short description/bio of the principal leaders : the powerful triumvirate of Stalin, Rykoff and Bukharin – against which are raged in opposition Trotzky, Zinovieff and Kameneff.
It also paints a picture of the poverty and unemployment of the town and urban Russians and contrasts this with the conditions of the peasants in the countryside; children & red hooligans, religion, marriage and family life, disease and the evils of bureaucracy.
Economic Difficulties “Russia is an undeveloped backward country; she is only potentially wealthy, and needs, therefore such assistance and stimulus as can transform her potential wealth into real wealth.
It requires individual enterprise but the Soviet system precludes all forms of individual enterprise, because it sees in it the enemy of Communism.
It needs assistance from without but the Soviet system deliberately antagonises and debars all foreign co-operation.”
Soviet Squabbles 1926 was not only a year of economic problems but was also even more remarkable for the discord among the actual leaders – lack of political unity through competing economic theory views and keen political rivalry that always exists among parvenu statesman.
It came to a head in an open struggle between Trotzky and the other leaders with Trotzky actually being dismissed from the post of Commander-in-Chief of the Red Forces, the Communist Party and exiled to the South.
Trotzky was reinstated but in a lesser position and with Zinovieff formed the first opposition since the Revolution.
Enemies of Britain The Soviets main objective abroad during this period was World Revolution – war against the Capitalists.
Great Britain was viewed as the Generalissimo of the Capitalist forces and so the destruction of British strength and prosperity by any and every means was a key objective.
Nine Red Days and After This refers to the period of the General Strike in Britain – the Soviets saw this as a prime opportunity to interfere and use the Miners’ strike to bring about a “British Revolution” and “Civil War” in Britain.
A lot of preparation was put in by the Communists in Russia to help facilitate such a Revolution (a Class War) and failed to see it as more of a domestic economic struggle/dispute !
It failed !!
The Eastern Peril “The movement in the East is turning the nucleus of the Revolution in Europe into World Revolution – Izvestya, 01/12/25”
The Soviets now turned their attention to dividing East and West – the liberation of the East from the oppression of the West.
Lenin himself drew up the Soviets’ Eastern Policy – using trained propagandists and agitators sent to various Eastern countries.
The object was firstly a Chinese Revolution.
The Red Objective “World Revolution” – targeted at Britain and the East !!

Soviet Dumping

A 40 page document written by Edouard with a foreword by The Rt. Hon. Lord Newton, P.C.,1 published by the Anglo-Russian Press Association in 1931 costing one shilling.

It is described on the title page as a summary of the economic war activities and the latest attempts made by the Soviet Government of Russia against the welfare of the civilised world.

Foreword

A very clear explanation of the concepts and dangers of ‘Soviet Dumping’.

Lord N highlights the dangers that the B political campaign against Britain would be supplemented by an industrial attack which, if successful, would go far towards undermining Britain’s economic position.

He describes Soviet Russia as a slave state, a gigantic internment camp in which 150 million humans are confined and the Government exercises despotic control…. the whole of the nation being effectively militarised, treating the workers as soldiers and dispatching them to which ever place their labour maybe required…. paid a minimum wage or nothing at all.

So the Soviet Govt enjoys the advantage of unlimited labour supply, immunity from strikes and an almost negligible cost of production.

…. thus the Soviet Govt has acquired factories, mines, forests, lands etc all for nothing by stealing them, can employ skilled foreign experts alongside mass labour at little cost. The result: they can dump goods in Europe at prices substantially lower than they are charging in Russia – the aim: to disorganise and disrupt international trade and prices , promote trade disputes abroad and increase unemployment levels in Europe – all helped in Britain by the Free Trade principles being followed. This could all help strengthen the spread of Bolshevism abroad – dangerous.

An International Problem

Soviet dumping threatens to undermine the whole fabric of international commerce and economics – selling goods to foreigners at a price far below that charged to their own people.

Commodities dumped by the Soviet Government are produced by the ‘omnipotent’ State on nationalised land or in confiscated factories, mines and works, or are obtained from its subjects at fixed prices regulated by the State and paid for in State printed currency which has no purchasing power outside the State. They are then transported on confiscated railways and inland water ways and loaded by convict, forced and ill-paid labour. The only real cost, in addition to the low cost of keeping the workers alive, is the charter of foreign ships in real foreign currency usually paid by the foreign buyer.

Example: In 1930 butter cost 19s 6d a pound in Moscow BUT sold in London for as low as 11 1/2d a pound !

Organised Dumping

This practice intensified in 1930 – exporting goods where there was a serious shortage in Russia itself, where the Russian people were in desperate need !]

Example: In 1930 the Soviets resumed the export of cereals on a large scale whilst at the same time establishing a strict system of rationing at home.

Dumping in Britain

Evidence is overwhelming.

Goods to the value of £165m are estimated to have been dumped into Britain during the period 1920 – 29.

Example: £4m of eggs dumped into Britain whilst the government ration for a fully qualified Communist is 1/3 of an egg per day !

Soviet commodities were sold at levels between 5% and 25% below all other prices.

Economic War

In 1918 all foreign trading throughout Russia was declared a State Monopoly – the Russian merchant effectively ceased to exist.

….. “the basis of our foreign trade policy is monopoly … and is one of the chief weapons for the regulation of our domestic market.”

The Foreign Trade Monopoly was to be used in the class war against the Capitalist system, the rich and protected countries … with Russian domestic industry at the same time being protected by high import tariffs.

Stalin is clever and well informed …. using the dumping approach he aims to create conditions of instability in world markets which he hopes may lead to world revolution.

British Trade and Russia

Soviet exports were financed by English banks for ¼ of the total value, ¼ as advances from brokers and a 1/3 of the remainder derived from other British sources – so 66% of the value of goods dumped in Britain were financed long before they even reached British shores.

Soviet Slavery

Estimated that the real value of the average weekly wage of the town worker amounts to 11s 3d a week – worse off than the average unemployed man with a wife and three children on unemployment pay in England !!

The Soviet Labour Code allowed the State to call up citizens between the ages of 18 and 45 for compulsory labour service – two years of continuous active work in industrial undertakings.

Unemployment was abolished as were public holidays, no freedom of movement of labour, women were mobilised and prison labour was utilised heavily.

Motives behind Dumping

Dumping was not new but Soviet dumping was different – aim to manipulate market relations, strike at capitalist stabilisation, disorganise the economic order – ultimately to assist in instigating World Revolution – whilst enslaving its own people.

Defensive Measures

The need to stop the financing of Soviet exports, stop the provision of the credits helping Stalin, increase the use of import controls and tariffs.

See Edouard’s booklet entitled “Can we trade with Russia?”.

Can we Trade with Russia?

A 48 page booklet written by Edouard in 1929, published by the Anglo-Russian Press costing one shilling.

It is described on the title page as : an analysis of Anglo-Soviet trade possibilities as revealed by the economic conditions of Soviet Russia, together with a suggestion for the legitimate extension of British exports.

Preface

Investigation concludes that “Soviet policy – and that alone – is responsible both for the present insignificant volume of trade and for the difficulties experienced whenever Britain has tried to revive and extend that trade.

The Dilemma

Political – how to establish normal commercial relations with a country that has openly proclaimed a state of militant antagonism towards the rest of the world?

Economic – how to adjust the economic machinery and methods obtaining in the rest of the world to the peculiar and unreciprocal conditions in Soviet Russia?

PLUS: Britain has been especially singled out by the Soviet Government as an objective of hatred and interference !!

Russia pleads for trade credits whilst refusing to settle old debts !

British Efforts

Many and varied attempts to try and ‘stretch out a helping hand to Russia’ – treaties, conferences, delegations, debt settlement proposals, offers of credits, freedom to establish trading companies in London etc, etc – at the British Government and individual trader level.

However, Russia now follows an economic despotism regime known as Foreign Trade Monopoly - i.e. all foreign trade is in the hands of the Soviet Government. – really another war measure against World Capitalism. The suspension of the law of supply and demand.

Aims at keeping foreign manufacturers and traders out of Russia, whilst claiming free entry and all facilities for free trading for itself and its officials in every other country in the world.

Russia requires the granting of trade credits but cannot show a sound economic or commercial position for this - defaulting on debts, breaking treaties, confiscating British properties and businesses in Russia.

Agriculture is still the only real basis of the country’s economy – industrialisation has not really taken off – home demand has collapsed due to the poverty of the people.

“We have electricity! We have ‘Lenin’s Lamp’ so that we can see ourselves starve”

However, the focus shifts to investing in building up heavy industry at the expense of light industry and the worsening conditions of the peasants – forced industrialisation takes all available money and credits whilst agricultural production falls and money is not available for everyday items!

Anglo-Soviet trade

Russia ends up importing less from all countries as she has less money to spend abroad – however, the British share of the loss has in fact been less than most other more favoured countries.

Trade Possibilities

Real trade orders in the Engineering, Chemical and Textile industries are possible but not thought to be large; hypothetical orders for industrial equipment would only be possible if Britain was prepared to grant huge loans and credits – but why should we do this?

Would it help reduce British unemployment numbers – even Churchill admits this would not actually occur – or at best would be a mere bagatelle in the face of 1 ½ million unemployed.

Statistics have been produced showing that the British public would need to put up £142 - !90m of credits in order to secure £30 to £40m of Russian orders to relieve unemployment !!

So how to extend trade?

Concludes that the state of the Russian economy is precarious and capacity to import freely is seriously diminished – the market is artificially controlled and Russia’s desire to industrialise itself dominates the whole programme of imports. Add to that the fact that Britain is an acknowledged enemy!

The Soviets sell products in Britain of £20m a year but only buy £3m a year.

The answer – an arrangement whereby exports of British products to Russia should be more or less equivalent to the value of Soviet exports to Britain plus a system whereby the Soviets have to deposit a % of the value of their exports to Britain as security for fulfilment of making British purchases – these deposits to be held in the British banking system.

Economic Russia: 1932: an authentic report for 1931 together with a review of Soviet plans and prospects for 1932

A 23 page booklet written by Edouard in 1932, published by the Anglo-Russian Press Association.

It provides “a general economic survey of Soviet Russia three years in to the Five Year Plan.”

It covers Industrial Production, Soviet Agriculture, Labour, Wages & Cost of Living, Foreign Trade and a summary of the Soviet economic programme for 1932.

Rather surprisingly for Edouard this booklet is more upbeat and positive about the Soviet economy and what has been achieved during 1931 and admits that significant progress has been made.

It concludes that although levels of production will be below plan in 1932, the last year of the Five Year Plan, these lower levels will be seen to be acceptable.

He then warns the reader that this level of progress is likely to result in Soviet Russia “gaining strength for her campaign of aggression against the rest of the world”!

with a Foreword by Lord Sydenham of Combe1

An extract from this foreword best describes the content and objective of this memorandum.

This is yet another ‘thought piece’ by Edouard on the complex subject of Anglo-Russian trade, an area of expertise for Edouard whose specialist subject can be seen to be economic trade – working with substantial amounts of raw data, showing his ability to analyse such primary data and present…

Of particular note and interest is Edouard’s radical proposition and theory that ‘ecomomic barter’ could be the solution to adjusting the trade balance between the two countries.

Chapter Heading Precis
Anglo-Soviet Trade “Soviet efforts focussed on increasing sales to Britain on the one hand and making insignificant purchases on the other.”
Shown clearly by the statistics that three years into the Five-Year Plan the Soviet surplus had increased to a level of £175m. This trend continued through the 11 year period 1924 – 1932.

The temporary Trade Agreement The Trade Agreement was very one-sided – and made no provision for the extension of British sales to Russia!
Indeed the Soviet Monopoly of Foreign Trade situation in existence in Russia actually prohibited British manufacturers and traders from creating and maintaining markets for their goods in Russia.
“Thus, in terminating the Temporary Agreement (1921 & 1930) the British Government has merely denounced an arrangement which operated in favour of only one party to the transaction.”
Gold payments The most dangerous aspect of Anglo-Soviet trade during that 11 year period was the large gold payments made by Britain to Russia.
Edouard argues that as the USA’s exports to Soviet Russia were four times larger than the imports – by Britain paying gold to Russia for their imports from Soviet Russia, Britain was effectively settling Russia’s account with the US!
Edouard makes the strong assertion that “Britain’s policy should be that trade must be balanced by goods and not by gold or credits”.

As indicated in Lord Sydenham’s foreword above Edouard’s last chapter is entitled Barter Trade. In this he contends that a new trade agreement should be put into place which must, once and for all, settle the question of trade reciprocity; it must provide for ….
